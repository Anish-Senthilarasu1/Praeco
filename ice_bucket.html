The Controversy of the Ice Bucket Challenge

Lydia Palatty July 5th, 2025

The University of Southern California's Ice Bucket Challenge took the internet by storm. Starting in California and spreading all the way to Texas, it aimed to raise mental health awareness. Nominated participants pour ice water over their heads to show their support before nominating their friends and family to join, popularizing the trend. But are nominees popularizing the challenge for the right reasons? In its shadow, the ALS Ice Bucket Challenge of 2014 lies in disregard. The original challenge was made to support the neurodegenerative disease, Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis, which still has no cure, so how can it be okay to turn it from a genuine cause to performance?

Wade Jefferson, a student at the University of South California, inspired by the loss of his friends to suicide, started the USC Ice Bucket Challenge in March 2025. It's also known as the USC #SpeakyourMIND challenge to spread awareness regarding mental health and support the non-profit Active Minds. Similarly, Pat Quinn and Pete Frates co-founded the ALS Ice Bucket Challenge in the summer of 2014 after being diagnosed with the disease to spread awareness and raise funds for treatment research, raising over $220 million. The USC challenge was a direct copy of the ALS version, just made to support mental health. This change has sparked controversy as many participants took away the meaning behind both versions, through unawareness, diminishing the little support the uncommon disease had.

ALS, also known as Lou Gehrig's disease, stands for Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis. Amyotrophic can be broken down into (a-) meaning not or without, (my/o) meaning muscle, and (-trophic) meaning growth, overall conveying no muscle growth. Lateral refers to the side of the spinal cord where the disease starts, and sclerosis means hardening. Altogether, the disease describes the hardening and decline of muscle growth. The disease causes your motor neurons, the neurons that enable muscle movement, to degenerate and die, leading to muscle weakness, paralysis, and eventually death. The reason the Ice Bucket Challenge has stronger ties to ALS than mental health is that dumping yourself with ice water was meant to—to a certain degree—replicate the physical sensation patients suffer daily, such as weakness and cramping in the hands, arms, and legs, shortness of breath, etc.

While mental health and ALS are both important to recognize, little is known about how to support ALS, and many people are unaware of its impact on a patient's life. Changing the meaning of the trend was not beneficial because it wasn't taken seriously to raise mental health awareness, and consequently, taking away from the original cause. People are forgetting what the challenge was meant for, taking away its impact. What is the point of "spreading awareness" if it detracts from a more critical aim?

The world's Instagram and TikTok feeds were filled with participants, but I have seen many who don't understand the meaning behind the challenge. Whether it was for ALS or mental health, the 2025 Ice Bucket Challenge was extremely performative, and as it spread, it did not make the impact it had the potential to. Many participants didn't understand or bother to learn the origin of the 2014 or 2025 versions, and had joined for the sake of posting. They didn't know about ALS, or even that the recent version was meant to encourage donating to Active Minds, a mental health nonprofit. While it's understandable and not uncommon to participate in various trends blindly, the USC Challenge was a wasted opportunity to bring awareness to mental health, and it took away from ALS, as it poorly addressed the issue.
