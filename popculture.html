Two Extremes: Pop Culture's Response to the Political Climate

[Author Name] February 15th 2025

On January 20th of 2025, Donald Trump was inaugurated into the oval office for his second term as President of the United States. On that same day, he issued 24 executive orders, which included withdrawing from the World Health Organization, declaring a national emergency at our southern border, and ending the recognition of anything beyond biological sex at birth on legal documentation. Today, President Trump has issued over 109 executive orders since the commencement of his second term, marking a staggering use of executive power that has rapidly reshaped the political climate.

Coincidentally, many major award shows have held their annual celebrations during Trump's second presidency, including The Academy Awards, The British Academy Film and Television Awards (BAFTAs), and The Grammys. As reflections of the current mainstream media, the results at these shows have revealed a clear trend: pop culture is retaliating against today's political climate. This dynamic is nothing new - history has shown time and again that when political leadership leans heavily toward one side of the ideological spectrum, mainstream culture pushes back with an equally forceful response in the opposite direction. From the countercultural movements of the 1960s reacting to conservative governance to the rise of politically charged art and entertainment during more right-wing administrations, pop culture has long served as a battleground for ideological and conceptual opposition. However, this cycle of extremes - where art and politics continuously clash rather than coexist - raises an important question: Is this polarization truly beneficial to society, or does it only deepen existing divides?

Take Emilia Pérez, for instance, a film directed by Jaques Audiard that saw much success at this year's awards shows. This film's bold political narrative lies in its unique storyline, where a Mexican cartel lawyer helps a drug lord transition into a woman and start a new life, tackling themes of gender identity, justice, and redemption. The Academy Awards and the BAFTAs, amongst many others, have nominated this film and awarded it with some of the most prestigious awards. This was much to the surprise of the general public, who questioned whether its recognition stemmed more from its political messaging than its artistic merit.

On TikTok and other social media platforms, users quickly voiced their skepticism, with many calling out what they saw as performative progressivism in Hollywood - where films that align with trending social ideologies are elevated, even if they polarize audiences or lack widespread appeal. The comment sections of various award show clips were flooded with debates, with some praising Emilia Pérez for its bold stance, while others accused the industry of sidelining more popular or critically acclaimed contenders like Wicked in favor of a political agenda. One TikTok user under the handle "jasongronau" summed up the frustration, saying, "Thank you to the Academy for this political and pitiful award. You snubbed the powerhouse performance that opened the show for this AI garbage." Whether sarcastic or sincere, reactions like this underscore the growing disconnect between cultural institutions and the public they aim to represent.

However, the root cause of this disconnect isn't simply bias within The Academy, but the increasingly charged political climate that surrounds it. In many ways, today's pop culture has become less about celebrating artistry and more about signaling, a direct response to the actions and rhetoric of Trump's administration. From redefining gender on legal documents to withdrawing from global institutions, Trump's second term has set a tone of ideological rigidity that artists (and awards shows) feel compelled to challenge. As a result, entertainment has transformed into a vehicle for resistance, where films, music, and media aren't just art, they're political statements. This transformation isn't inherently negative - art has always reflected society's tensions - but the speed and intensity of this shift have made it harder to separate cultural expression from political allegiance. What we're seeing isn't just commentary, but combat.

This ideological tug-of-war between pop culture and politics has become so ingrained in our world that it seems like a natural evolution of a free society. But, when every award, performance, or casting choice becomes a battleground for ideals, it creates fatigue for the onlookers and deepens cultural division. Instead of uniting society through shared stories and experiences, art becomes a proxy war for ideological control. For younger generations, especially those still forming their identities and beliefs, this constant polarization creates a confusing and alienating environment. Pop culture is supposed to reflect the complexity and nuance of our real world, not just serve as a megaphone for political agendas, regardless of where on the political spectrum those agendas lie. When every film is watched like a campaign speech and every song is interpreted as a protest, we limit the space for nuance, curiosity, and genuine dialogue.

So, where does that leave us? If the political world continues to drive us even further into the conservative extreme, and culture continues to respond with equally extreme reactions, then the challenge for us, as the audience, is to demand more balance. To create, critique, and consume art that isn't afraid to ask questions rather than deliver answers. To recognize when something is truly brave and innovative, and when it's just designed to stir controversy. Polarization may fuel headlines, but in the long run, it's the thoughtful and authentic storytelling of true artistry, not the reactionary signalling being honored today, that will shape a culture worth celebrating.
