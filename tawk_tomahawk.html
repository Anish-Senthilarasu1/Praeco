Monthly Melomania

Tawk Tomahawk — Hiatus Kaiyote

Anonymous

If I had a quarter for every Australian artist I listened to, I'd have 50 cents. I can thank Hiatus Kaiyote for half of that money. Around 2010, in Melbourne Australia, Naomi Saalfield (commonly known as Nai Palm) pursued a career as a solo guitarist. She was approached by Paul Bender after one of her shows, he offered to collaborate with her. Not long after, the duo picked up keyboardist Simon Mavin and drummer Perrin Moss, forming the band Hiatus Kaiyote. Two years later, the band released their debut album: Tawk Tomahawk.

If you asked me to define this record by a single genre, I'd be at a loss. Each song feels as though it belongs to a separate album. The tracks are a mix of jazz, rock psychedelia, neo soul and many other genres I'm unable to put a name to. Hiatus Kaiyote doesn't approach their music in a conventional way. In fact, many songs on this record have esoteric lyrics that to this day I don't understand. However, the vocal delivery is so stellar that it makes up for the zany content.

The first track, Mobius Streak, was an amazing introduction to this album. The use of keys and synth in this song is genuinely impressive. At the same time, Nai Palm's vocals complement the instrumental: it doesn't feel like she's singing to the melody; instead, her voice blends into the arrangement as a fellow instrument. This song is 6 minutes long, but while listening, it doesn't feel half. It's a unique, eccentric track and I have yet to hear anything sound similar to it.

Lace Skull is the 7th song off this album, and it's easily my favorite. This track is awe-inspiring, there are so many things that I love about it. Notably, it doesn't stay in one spot, the tone and tempo constantly shift. Nai Palm put a piece of her soul in this song while recording it, her vocal performance was moving. Paul Bender also played a large role by creating a beautifully complementary bass line. Every member of the band did an absolutely amazing job in making this song incredible.

There isn't much that I dislike about this record, but the song Boom Child was a low point for me. Every track besides this one had at least one element that made it unique or interesting in a way. I feel as though there was more they could have done for this song. It's a very bare-bones track, at least when compared to every other melody on the album. However, the song is less than two minutes long so it doesn't last longer than it needs to.

While I only chose to talk about these three noteworthy tracks, the remainder of the album is just as creatively significant. There are more songs that I really enjoyed. The World It Softly Lulls, Rainbow Rhodes and Leap Frog are all great songs that I find myself returning to. This project brings lulling melodies, refreshing beat experiments, and powerful instrumental performances — all reasons to give it a listen. This record is a solid 9/10 for me.
