Life is not a Cabaret, Old Chum: On Denial, Spectacle, and American Decay

Rabia Ghanchi August 10th, 2025

Cabaret has been and continues to be the sensationally masterful production it was when it hit the Boston, Shubert Theatre in October of 1966. Set in Berlin, Germany before the Nazi takeover in the 1930's, the story follows an American writer, Clifford Bradshaw, who arrived in Germany to clear his head and become inspired by Berlin's bustling nightlife, filled to the brim with hedonism and self-pleasure. At the Kit Kat Klub, hosted by the flirtatious and alluring Emcee, or the Master of Ceremonies, Bradshaw meets Sally Bowles, an English performer at the Klub. Months later, the couple have grown to love and cherish one another deeply. This however, takes a turn as the Nazist ideologies—which initially began with a slow meandering—now ran rampant across the country. At an engagement party between Fraulein Schneider and her fiancee, Herr Schultz, a jewish man, proud Nazi and swastika-sporting Ernst warns Fraulein Schneider of her unwiseness.

By this point in the production, the audience should feel an impending doom barrelling right at them, kicking them in the nose with a bloody mayhem. The Emcee, who started seductive and bubbly turned to cater to the Nazis which would later take over. His teasing musical numbers, now transformed into something as a cause for concern, a means of pro-Nazi propaganda as the songs become war-like chants instead of the jazzy flash which they had once been. He performs the song "If You Could See Her" where he dances with a woman in a gorilla costume. Funny, a man who claims to be in love with a gorilla while they twirl in whimsical patterns across a stage while singing seemingly loving lyrics—like a child in a fantastical dream.

"Yet when we're walking together
They sneer if I'm holding her hand
But if they could see her through my eyes
Maybe they'd all understand." (Masteroff, 76)

The Emcee then unleashes the last lines of the song as their dance dwindles to an end, stinging like a searing burn in the back of your throat, realizing how truly disgusting and dehumanizing the song really turned out to be.

"Is it a crime to fall in love?
Why can't the world "Leben und Leben lassen"—
Live and let live?
But if you could see her through my eyes…
She wouldn't look Jewish at all." (76–77)

Ernst, Bradshaw's first friend on his railroad train to Berlin, leaves him with no choice. While thunder and lightning crashes outside the boarding, Bradshaw stays with Sally, he demands her to pack her things, saying he's moving back to the United States and that he's taking Sally with her. The Nazi regime has made waves beyond safety, and he remained absolutely shaken after his brief interaction with Ernst at the party about the swastika armband he proudly carried on him. They argue, Sally staking her feet in the ground and demanding she stay in Berlin, where she works and lives. After all, Bradshaw wasn't meant to be in her life for so long as she should have moved on from him by now and him leaving would make it easier for her to do so. Bradshaw ignores her pleas and says living in America with him would keep her safe from the dangers that would beat on soon in Berlin, that they could be happy if she just learned to cooperate.

Then, Sally says something that would radiate within the audience since her character first utters it on stage in 1966, continuing to undulate ever since.

"It's only politics, and what's that to do with us?" (79)

Every production of Cabaret works around the premise of this question, no matter which director takes it head on, and every time Sally Bowles utters this line, it lands like a punch—precisely because audiences, back in 1966 and even now, know just how deadly apathy can be. We even side with Bradshaw when he goes ballistic on Sally:

"You're right! Nothing has anything to do with us! Sally—you don't understand—if you're not against all this—you're for it. Or you might as well be!" (79)
—

On July 1st, a military-grade prison known as Alligator Alcatraz was opened to house migrant detainees who arrived after three days of its official opening with the jovial happiness of a ribbon-cutting ceremony exuding from the Trump administration. A day after the grand opening, the prison already began to flood as it was built in only eight days. Despite this, truckloads of detainees were rounded up and poured into Alligator Alcatraz with no access to outside communication.

On July 4th, 2025—Independence Day—President Trump passed the 'One Big Beautiful Bill'. Surrounded by constituents and political affiliates, applause and laughter filled the Oval Office as he signed the bill passed to him by the House just the day before. Teetering at just over one thousand pages, the bill will create the largest historical fund-cut to Medicaid, resulting in 16 million Americans losing their health insurance, 186 billion dollars in cuts to the food stamps SNAP programs for 4.3 million people, and will take away free and reduced lunch for 16 million students in the K-12 public school system, while giving billionaires massive tax breaks. The bill drowns the United States further into national debt, adding 3.4 trillion dollars to deficits and has now been considered the largest transfer of money from the poor to the rich in history.

As America drifts further into a politics of cruelty disguised as entertainment, Cabaret no longer feels like a story of the past. It feels like a warning.

—
"Leave your troubles… outside! Life is disappointing, we get it! We have no problems here, in here, life… is beautiful!" (2)

The Emcee allures the audience with these lines in between sultry lyrics in the song "Wilkommen", where he invites the audience to take a seat, have a drink, and enjoy the spectacle that would soon follow in the Kit Kat Klub. He creates a safe haven for visitors, shielding them from the horrors outside, at the same time, trapping them from ever knowing anything at all. The Klub convinces you that what happens outside, stays outside. Politics stays politics and it has nothing to do with you. Whenever the story enters the part of the plotline where it appears the Nazi regime is too close for comfort, there is no reason to worry, as Emcee is just a moment away to perform another musical number that will lift spirits in no time.

However, there is one thing to consider. No matter how much we convince ourselves that we side with Bradshaw's response, to some extent, we are all Sally. As long as we distance ourselves from the knowledge of what goes on around the world, we'll be safe. Ignorance is bliss, right?

Hiding behind the spectacles of the internet is so entrancing, much more attractive than facing injustices up front and personal—as long as you are not the one being directly affected. But we're too busy. Too busy arguing over whether Sabrina Carpenter's album cover is feminist. Too busy rehashing 2018 celebrity drama: Hailey copied Selena. Selena copied Hailey. Meanwhile, a young woman in Gaza picks up the pieces of a family shattered by U.S.-funded bombings. But that doesn't trend, does it? Your neighbor was detained by ICE? That's unfortunate, but let's dissect Kylie Jenner's nose job!

Nevertheless, indulge! Life is too short to spend it on empathy, right? As Sally says, "Life is a cabaret, old chum! Come to the cabaret!"
