Artists of the Soul

Maya Jabbar July 7th, 2025

The Art of Humanity:

Every soul carries a canvas.

Some more vibrant, splashed with open hearts and brushstrokes of shared sorrows, colors that blur the line between your pain and mine. Others hold softer tones, where distance remains but compassion still paints a quiet, observing light. And then there are canvases left nearly blank, brushes untouched, emotions sealed away like colors never mixed.

To me these aren't just feelings. They are empathy, sympathy and apathy; three distinct palettes and three ways of responding to the world's endless exhibition of emotion. One dives into the frame, another watches from afar, and the last turns away unmoved. But the beauty and the burden of being human is that we choose which palette to dip into. We control each brushstroke with every interaction, artists of emotion crafting portraits of understanding or detachment, depth or distance.

The Art of Empathy:

Empathy is the boldest brushstroke one can use from the emotional gallery. It doesn't just observe, it climbs into the canvas and walks through the shadows and light, touching the texture of someone else's pain or joy as if it were its own.

To be empathetic is to paint with borrowed colors. You feel what another feels, not because you've been told to, but instead because your heart mirrors their hues. It's a kind of magic—when your sorrow becomes mine, when your laughter echoes in my chest.

Artists know this instinctively. A good portrait isn't just a likeness—it holds the spirit of its subject. Just like a person with empathy doesn't just say, "I see you're hurting," but instead, "I hurt with you."

Strokes of empathy take courage. It asks us to set aside our own palette and mix unfamiliar shades. It asks us to imagine the chaos behind someone's smile, the storm behind their silence. It's a compassionate, vulnerable act of emotional immersion like dipping your feet into the unknown.

And while it's the most powerful of the emotional arts, it's also the most exhausting. Empathy can wear you down, blur your own edges. But with balance, it builds the most human of masterpieces: connection.

So if you find yourself standing before another's unspoken pain, don't just offer a glance. Pick up your brush, step into their world and let your soul echo theirs, even if only for a moment.

The Art of Sympathy:

Sympathy is a light brushstroke—soft, cautious, and careful not to disturb the canvas too much. It hovers just above the emotion, blending pastel tones of concern without diving into the deeper pigments of pain. Depicting the message, "I see your sadness," but from the side, not the center.

Sympathy doesn't claim to know what it feels like—it simply wants to soothe. The artist's gestures are like a warm wash of color: a note left on a desk, a quiet nod, a hand resting lightly on the shoulder. It doesn't try to repaint the scene. It just adds a gentle layer of comfort over the chaos.

But sympathy, when insincere or careless, can turn too translucent, like the water we clean our brushes with—barely leaving a mark at all. It risks becoming performative, a polite dab of emotion with no texture nor meaning behind it. The art lies in keeping it soothing, yet intentional. It is to say, "I may not feel exactly what you do, but I care enough to stand beside your portrait for a while."

In the gallery of feeling, sympathy is the soft stroke that doesn't alter the original image, but frames it with grace.

The Art of Apathy:

Apathy is the space where the brush never touches. A bare corner on the canvas. It is the decision not to feel, the refusal to dip into the colors of someone else's reality. There is no brushstroke of concern, no shadow of curiosity, no attempt. Apathy doesn't smudge or blend; it simply steps away. In its place, a blank canvas of emptiness and indifference.

Sometimes, apathy is born from exhaustion. When a soul has painted too many feelings, too often, it may put the brushes down—not from cruelty, but from fatigue, their canvas worn thin. Other times, apathy grows from disconnection, a silent belief that this—whatever it is—doesn't matter, doesn't deserve a response.

In a world overwhelmed with emotion, apathy can feel like a shield. But when left unchecked, it becomes a wall—thick, impassable, and lonely. It removes us from the artwork of humanity, leaving us as bystanders in a gallery of living people, feeling nothing at all.

And yet, even the blankest space can change. All it takes is a crack—one moment, one glance, one color—to remind us that even the most abandoned canvas can be filled again. Apathy is not the end of the painting, only a pause. The brush waits nearby.

The question is: will you pick it up?

The Gallery of Art:

As artists take their final strokes you notice that we are not remembered just by the words we spoke or the things we did—but by the way we made others feel. We are walking galleries of emotional art—every look, every hug, every silence leaves a mark on someone's soul.

Empathy colors the world with understanding. Sympathy brings softness where we can't quite reach. And apathy—while powerful—reminds us what's at stake when we stop painting at all.

As an artist you must be mindful of your strokes. Embrace boldness when it's time to feel deeply. Be gentle when someone just needs a presence. And don't be afraid to rest the brush—but never abandon the canvas.

Because to feel is not a weakness, in fact, it's the most human art of all.
